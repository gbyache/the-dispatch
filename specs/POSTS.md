# The Dispatch — Post Framework
## POSTS.md v1.0

---

## 1. MICHAEL LAWANSON — VOICE

**Who he is:** Brand strategist and designer. Works at the intersection of brand identity, positioning, and visual systems.

**Tone:** Calm, observational, direct. Starts with an industry pattern or a common mistake, builds to a clear insight. No hype. No self-promotion. No hashtags. No emoji. No exclamation points.

**Format:** 4–6 short paragraphs. Each one earns the next. The last line is the payoff — make it land.

**What the reader walks away with:** A sharper way of thinking about brand work. They feel like they've been told the truth about something the industry dances around.

---

## 2. MICHAEL LAWANSON — CONTENT PILLARS

### Pillar 1 — Strategy & Design (Mon + Wed)
The space where business strategy and visual identity meet. Posts in this pillar challenge bad briefs, expose the limits of aesthetics-first thinking, and name the patterns that lead to brand work that doesn't hold up.

**Topics that work:**
- Why rebrands fail before a single sketch is made
- The difference between a brand voice and brand decisions
- What "make it pop" actually means (and why it's a red flag)
- Why beautiful work built on a bad brief still fails
- The real reason identity systems lose coherence over time

### Pillar 2 — Mikey's Gourmet Chin Chin (Friday)
Michael's food brand. Small-batch chin chin, mother's recipe, Lagos roots, made in LA. Posts in this pillar tell the brand's story — the cultural gap it fills, the category it's creating, the craft behind it.

**Topics that work:**
- The cultural context chin chin has always had (and why the brand category ignored it)
- What "category of one" actually means for a product like this
- The craft and sourcing story
- Milestone updates: stockists, events, availability
- The gap between cultural presence and brand attention

## 3. WOLF & PIGEONS — VOICE

**Who they are:** A sports and entertainment brand design agency. They think in visual systems, brand identity, and what makes a sports brand last beyond wins and losses.

**Tone:** Industry insider. Slightly critical. Precise. Posts read like someone who has been in the room when decisions like these get made and has seen which ones age well.

**Format:** Same as Michael — 4–6 paragraphs, last line lands. No hashtags. No emoji.

---

## 4. WOLF & PIGEONS — CONTENT PILLARS

### Pillar 1 — Industry Reaction (Monday)
Reacting to something happening in sports or entertainment brand world. Naming rights deals, rebrands, uniform launches, team identity moves. Not a recap — a take.

**Angle:** What does this reveal about how organizations actually treat brand equity? What did they get right or wrong? What does it mean for the category?

### Pillar 2 — Design Craft (Wednesday)
The technical and strategic craft of sports brand design. Color systems, typography at scale, logo hierarchy, live environment vs. digital environment design.

**Angle:** Things that look simple but aren't. Decisions that only matter when you understand the full context. What separates a good mark from a great one.

### Pillar 3 — Brand Philosophy (Friday)
The deeper question: what is a sports brand actually for? Posts in this pillar zoom out from execution and ask what makes a brand mean something beyond the results on the field.

**Angle:** Off-season brand strength. Fan belonging. The difference between brand awareness and brand meaning. What holds up when the team is losing.

## 5. POST QUALITY CHECKLIST

Before finalizing any post:

- [ ] Could this come from any other brand strategist or agency? If yes — rewrite. It needs to be specific.
- [ ] Does the last line land? Read it in isolation. If it doesn't hit, the post doesn't work.
- [ ] Is there any filler? ("It's important to note that...") Cut it.
- [ ] Does it make a real claim? Or is it just restating things everyone already knows?
- [ ] No hashtags, no emoji, no exclamation points.
- [ ] Under 3,000 characters (LinkedIn limit is 3,000).
